You are drafting the Section 9 narrative for Form 656 (Offer in Compromise). This is the single most important written component of the OIC submission — the document the IRS examiner will read to decide whether your client's offer is worth accepting.

PURPOSE: Convince the examiner that the offered amount represents the maximum the government can expect to collect, and that acceptance is in the government's interest. This isn't a legal brief — it's a story about a real person told through the lens of IRC § 7122.

WRITE THIS AS FLOWING PROSE. No headers. No bullet points. No section numbers. It gets pasted directly into Section 9 of Form 656. Begin with the first sentence of the narrative.

PARAGRAPH THEMES (these guide the content, not the structure — the narrative should flow naturally):

1. THE CIRCUMSTANCES: How did this happen? Open with the human story. A dental practice that couldn't weather the pandemic. A divorce that left one spouse with all the liability and none of the assets. A medical crisis that derailed a career. Be specific — dates, events, amounts. The examiner reads dozens of these; the ones with real detail are the ones that land.

2. THE FINANCIAL REALITY: Paint the current picture. Not a Form 433 recitation — a synthesis. "The Whitfields' combined monthly income of $X barely covers their IRS-allowable living expenses of $Y, leaving $Z in monthly disposable income." Explain why this situation is stable, not temporary — the examiner needs to believe this IS the financial reality, not a snapshot that will improve.

3. ASSETS IN CONTEXT: The examiner will scrutinize assets. Get ahead of it. If there's a retirement account, explain why liquidation would cause hardship (10% penalty, tax consequences, retirement insecurity). If there's real property, explain the equity position. If there's a business, explain why it can't be liquidated without destroying the taxpayer's earning capacity. Each significant asset should have a "yes, we know about this, and here's why it doesn't change the RCP."

4. WHY THE OFFER IS RIGHT: Connect the dots without doing a formal RCP calculation (that's the working papers' job). "Given the limited disposable income, modest asset equity, and the approaching CSEDs, the offered amount of $X represents the maximum collectible within the statutory period." Plain language. Factual basis. Logical conclusion.

5. COMPLIANCE & GOOD FAITH: All returns are filed. Estimated payments are current. The taxpayer is represented by a licensed practitioner. Future compliance is assured. This paragraph turns a debtor into a future compliant taxpayer — which is what the IRS actually wants.

7. THE CLOSE: Acceptance serves the government's interest. The alternative — years of ACS collection activity, possible installment default, eventual CSED expiration — produces less revenue than the offered amount accepted now. One clear sentence on why the math favors acceptance.

TONE: Professional and respectful. This is a request, not an argument. Factual, not emotional — but human. The strength comes from specific facts connected to the legal standard, not from rhetoric. Let the story do the work.

LENGTH: 500-1000 words. Every sentence should earn its place.
