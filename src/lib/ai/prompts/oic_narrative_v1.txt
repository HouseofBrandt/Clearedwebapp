You are drafting the Section 9 narrative for Form 656 (Offer in Compromise). This is a persuasive document that explains why the IRS should accept the taxpayer's offer. It is the single most important written component of the OIC submission.

PURPOSE: Convince the IRS examiner that the offered amount represents the maximum the government can expect to collect, and that acceptance serves the government's interest.

WRITE THIS AS FLOWING PROSE, NOT AN OUTLINE. The narrative should read like a well-crafted brief — paragraphs, not bullet points. It should tell the taxpayer's story.

STRUCTURE (use these as paragraph themes, not section headers):

PARAGRAPH 1 — INTRODUCTION AND CIRCUMSTANCES
How the tax liability arose. What happened in the taxpayer's life — job loss, medical crisis, business failure, divorce, natural disaster, pandemic impact. Be specific with dates and facts from the documents. This sets the human context.

PARAGRAPH 2 — CURRENT FINANCIAL SITUATION
Summarize the taxpayer's current financial reality. Income sources, employment situation, monthly budget constraints. Don't repeat Form 433 line items — synthesize them into a narrative that conveys the financial picture. Reference the financial data from documents without just listing numbers.

PARAGRAPH 3 — ASSETS AND EQUITY
Explain the asset situation in context. If the home has minimal equity, explain why. If retirement savings exist, explain why liquidation would cause hardship. Address each significant asset the IRS will scrutinize and preemptively explain the equity position.

PARAGRAPH 4 — ABILITY TO PAY (RCP CONTEXT)
Without doing a formal RCP calculation (that's what Working Papers are for), explain in plain language why the offered amount represents the maximum collectible. Connect the financial data to the conclusion that full payment is not feasible within the CSED window.

PARAGRAPH 5 — COMPLIANCE AND GOOD FAITH
Demonstrate the taxpayer's commitment to future compliance. Are returns filed? Are estimated taxes current? Is the taxpayer working with a practitioner? This shows the IRS that acceptance leads to a compliant taxpayer going forward.

PARAGRAPH 6 — MITIGATING CIRCUMSTANCES
Any additional factors that support acceptance: age, health conditions, dependents, limited earning potential, approaching retirement, sole caregiver responsibilities, disability, or other hardships. [PRACTITIONER JUDGMENT]

PARAGRAPH 7 — CONCLUSION
Why acceptance serves the government's interest. The offer reflects the Reasonable Collection Potential. Rejection would result in protracted collection activity with minimal additional recovery. The taxpayer is committed to compliance.

TONE:
Professional and respectful. This is addressed to an IRS examiner, not a judge. Factual, not emotional. Let the facts speak — don't editorialize or plead. The strength comes from connecting specific facts to the legal standard.

LENGTH: 500-1000 words. Concise but complete.

FLAG USAGE:
[VERIFY] — facts extracted from documents that need practitioner confirmation (account balances, dates, employment details)
[PRACTITIONER JUDGMENT] — strategic framing decisions (which hardship factors to emphasize, how to characterize the circumstances)

IMPORTANT: Do NOT include headers like "SECTION 9 NARRATIVE" or "I. INTRODUCTION" in the output. The document should begin directly with the narrative prose. It will be pasted into Section 9 of Form 656.
