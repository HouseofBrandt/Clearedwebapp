## 4. WILLFULNESS ANALYSIS

The second element. Willfulness doesn't require evil intent — it means a voluntary, conscious, and intentional act of preferring other creditors over the IRS. Paying rent while not paying payroll taxes = willful. The bar is low, but there are real defenses.

**The Facts That Matter:**

Present this as a narrative, not a table. Walk through the timeline:
- When did the trust fund taxes first go unpaid? [VERIFY]
- When did the client first learn (or should have learned) of the delinquency?
- What did the client do after learning? (This is critical — affirmative steps to remedy weigh against willfulness)
- Were other creditors paid during the delinquent periods? [VERIFY: bank statements showing payments to vendors, rent, utilities while 941s went unpaid]
- Was there a reasonable belief that the taxes were being handled? By whom?

**Defense Arguments:** [PRACTITIONER JUDGMENT]

Identify every viable willfulness defense and assess its strength honestly:

- **Reasonable reliance on others:** "[NAME] delegated payroll to [BOOKKEEPER], who had handled it without issue for 7 years. [NAME] had no reason to suspect non-compliance until [triggering event]." Strong when the delegation was to a qualified person and there were no red flags. Weak if the client ignored warning signs. See Newsome v. United States, 431 F.2d 742 (5th Cir. 1970). [VERIFY: citation]
- **No knowledge:** The client genuinely didn't know taxes were unpaid. Viable if the client wasn't involved in finances and wasn't on notice. Destroyed if the client received IRS notices or had access to bank statements showing the shortfall.
- **Encumbered funds:** All available funds were subject to a prior lien or court order. Narrow defense — the encumbrance must have existed before the tax obligation arose.
- **Reasonable cause:** Unusual circumstances prevented payment despite efforts to comply. Think natural disaster, bank failure, embezzlement by a trusted employee.

**Willfulness Assessment:** [PRACTITIONER JUDGMENT] Be direct. "The willfulness defense is the stronger argument here — [NAME]'s lack of financial involvement and reasonable reliance on [BOOKKEEPER] give us a credible story. But the two IRS CP504 notices sent to [NAME]'s home address in Q2 2023 are a problem. If the IRS can show [NAME] received those notices and still didn't act, the reliance defense collapses."

## 6. APPEAL & LITIGATION OPTIONS

Brief but complete:
- **Pre-assessment protest:** If the TFRP hasn't been assessed yet (Letter 1153 stage), the client can protest to IRS Appeals. Deadline: 60 days from Letter 1153. [VERIFY: has Letter 1153 been issued? What's the deadline?]
- **Post-assessment:** Pay the tax for one employee for one quarter, file a refund claim, then sue in District Court or Court of Federal Claims. Flora full-payment rule does not apply to TFRP — divisible tax exception. See Flora v. United States, 362 U.S. 145 (1960). [VERIFY: citation]
- **CDP hearing:** If a lien or levy notice has been issued, Collection Due Process hearing is available. IRC § 6320/6330.
- **Statute of limitations for collection:** 10-year CSED from date of assessment applies to the TFRP just like any other assessed tax. [VERIFY: assessment dates and CSEDs]
