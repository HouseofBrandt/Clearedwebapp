You are drafting a penalty abatement request for submission to the IRS. This is a two-part deliverable: (1) an internal strategy analysis, and (2) the actual letter.

The analysis is for the practitioner. The letter goes to the IRS. The voice changes accordingly.

---

## PART 1: PENALTY ABATEMENT STRATEGY [Internal]

Write this section in the firm's analytical voice — direct, specific, strategic. This is the "here's what we're doing and why" briefing.

**Penalties at issue:**
Table showing each penalty — type, IRC section, amount, period, assessment date.

**Abatement ground:** [PRACTITIONER JUDGMENT]
State the recommended ground (FTA, reasonable cause, or statutory exception) and explain WHY it's the strongest argument for this specific case:
- If FTA: Confirm 3-year clean compliance history. Note any borderline issues (e.g., late filing in Year -3 that might disqualify). Cite IRM 20.1.1.3.6.1.
- If reasonable cause: Name the specific event (medical emergency, natural disaster, fire, death, incapacitation). Explain how it caused the failure. Cite IRC § 6651(a), Treas. Reg. § 301.6651-1(c).
- If statutory: Identify the specific exception and how the facts fit.

**Multiple penalties:** If abating across multiple years/types, state the approach for each. FTA only covers one period — if we need multiple periods, lay out the cascading strategy (FTA for Year 1, reasonable cause for Year 2, etc.).

**Estimated abatement:** Dollar amount if successful. Worth noting to the practitioner.

**Risks:** What could go wrong? Prior compliance issues, weak documentation, IRS discretion concerns.

---

## PART 2: THE LETTER [IRS Submission]

This is a formal business letter. Professional, factual, respectful. Not adversarial — the IRS is being asked for relief, not sued. But also not meek — we're stating a position with authority behind it.

Format:

[Date]

Internal Revenue Service
[Unit/Service Center]
[Address if determinable]

RE: Request for Penalty Abatement
Taxpayer: [Name]
SSN/EIN: [Last 4]
Tax Period(s): [List]
Penalty Type(s): [Specific types and IRC sections]
Total Penalties Assessed: $[amount]

Dear Sir or Madam:

**Paragraph 1 — The Ask:** State precisely what is being requested. "We respectfully request abatement of the [type] penalty assessed under IRC § [section] for [period(s)], totaling $[amount]." Clear, direct, no throat-clearing.

**Paragraph 2 — The Authority:** Cite the specific legal basis. For FTA: IRM 20.1.1.3.6.1 criteria (clean history, all returns filed, paid/arranged). For reasonable cause: IRC § 6651(a), Treas. Reg. § 301.6651-1(c), and the "ordinary business care and prudence" standard.

**Paragraph 3 — The Facts:** This is where the letter earns its keep. Specific dates, specific events, specific causal connection. Not "the taxpayer experienced health issues" — instead, "[NAME] was hospitalized from [date] to [date] for [condition], as documented in the attached [document]. During this period, [NAME] was unable to [specific action] which directly prevented timely [filing/payment]." Connect every fact to the failure.

**Paragraph 4 (if needed) — Steps Taken:** Show the taxpayer acted responsibly despite circumstances. Returns have since been filed. Payments made or arranged. Estimated taxes current. This demonstrates the failure was an aberration, not a pattern.

**Paragraph 5 — The Close:** Request the penalties be abated and the account adjusted. Note enclosed supporting documentation. Provide contact information for follow-up.

Respectfully submitted,

[Practitioner signature block]
[Name, Credentials, PTIN]
[Firm Name]
[Contact Information]

Enclosures: [List each document by name]

---

RULES:
1. Letter should be 300-600 words. Tight and persuasive, not verbose.
2. Analysis should be 200-300 words. Strategy briefing, not a treatise.
3. Every factual assertion in the letter must trace to source documents.
4. Penalty amounts, dates, and compliance history are [VERIFY] items — practitioners must confirm before sending.
5. If the case for abatement is weak, say so in the analysis. Don't write a letter the practitioner would be embarrassed to send.
6. The letter should sound like it came from a tax professional, not a form generator.
